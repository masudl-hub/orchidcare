1. What's in the box

ESP32 Dev Board

Small blue/black board with a micro-USB port. This is the brain — it has WiFi, reads the sensors, and POSTs data to your server.

Capacitive Soil Moisture Sensor

Long flat probe with a small circuit board at the top. 3 pins: VCC, GND, AOUT. Measures how wet the soil is.

DHT11 Module

Small blue square on a breakout board. 3 pins: + (VCC), - (GND), S or DATA. Measures air temperature and humidity.

BH1750 Light Sensor

Tiny purple/blue board. 4-5 pins: VCC, GND, SDA, SCL (and maybe ADDR). Measures light level in lux.

Breadboard

White/clear plastic rectangle with a grid of holes. Lets you connect everything without soldering.

Jumper Wires

Colorful wires with metal pins on each end. You'll need 12 total. Use colors to keep things organized.

You also need a micro-USB cable (the same kind that charges older Android phones) to connect the ESP32 to your laptop.

2. How YOUR breadboard works

Your breadboard: columns A-J, rows 1-30, with + and - rails on both sides.

  LEFT           MAIN GRID                RIGHT
  RAILS   A  B  C  D  E │ F  G  H  I  J  RAILS
  ─────   ───────────────┼───────────────  ─────
  + | -   o  o  o  o  o  │  o  o  o  o  o  + | -    Row 1
  + | -   o  o  o  o  o  │  o  o  o  o  o  + | -    Row 2
  + | -   o  o  o  o  o  │  o  o  o  o  o  + | -    Row 3
   ...     ...           │           ...    ...
  + | -   o  o  o  o  o  │  o  o  o  o  o  + | -    Row 30
The golden rule: Holes in the same row, same side of the center gap, are connected. A5 B5 C5 D5 E5 = all connected. F5 G5 H5 I5 J5 = all connected. But left side and right side are NOT connected to each other. The + rail holes are all connected vertically. Same for the - rail.

3. Place the ESP32

STEP

Straddle the center gap, rows 1-15

Push the ESP32 into the breadboard with its left pins in column B and right pins in column J. USB port faces up (off the top edge).

Your ESP32 is wide! The board covers columns C through I. Only column A is free (next to the left-side pins). The right-side pins in column J have no free neighbor.

• Left-side pins — connect via column A (male-male wires into the breadboard)
• Right-side pins — connect via male-female wires that clip directly onto the pin sticking up above the board

Two types of wires you'll use:
• Male-male (pin↔pin) — for left-side ESP32 pins (poke into column A) and for sensor modules in rows 16-30
• Male-female (pin↔socket) — the socket clips onto right-side ESP32 pins (D34, GND at J2) that have no free breadboard hole. The pin end goes wherever you need it.

4. Wire the power rails

Two wires to get power flowing to the left-side rails (we'll use these for all sensors).

WIRE 1

A1 → left + rail (male-male)

Red wire. Poke one end into A1 (same row as 3V3 at B1 — they're connected). Poke the other end into the left + (red) rail.

WIRE 2

A2 → left - rail (male-male)

Black wire. Poke one end into A2 (same row as GND at B2). Poke the other end into the left - (blue) rail.

Checkpoint: The left + rail is now 3.3V. The left - rail is now ground. Every sensor will tap into these two rails.

5. Wire the soil moisture sensor

The soil sensor is a probe on a cable ending in 3 female sockets. It came with small straight header pins (little metal prongs). Push those into the female sockets — they'll stay snug. Then plug the header pins into the breadboard.

PLACE

Insert header pins into the soil sensor's sockets, then plug into the breadboard

Push header pins into all 3 female sockets on the sensor cable. Then insert into C18 (VCC), C19 (GND), C20 (AOUT). Check your sensor's pin labels — order may vary!

WIRE 3

Soil VCC → left + rail (male-male)

Red wire from A18 (same row as VCC) to the left + rail.

WIRE 4

Soil GND → left - rail (male-male)

Black wire from A19 (same row as GND) to the left - rail.

WIRE 5

Soil AOUT → A6 (GPIO 35) (male-male)

Orange wire from A20 (same row as AOUT) to A6. Why A6? Because B6 = RX2/GPIO 35, and A6 is the free hole in that row. GPIO 35 is on ADC1, same as GPIO 34 — works perfectly for soil moisture.

6. Wire the DHT11 (temp + humidity)

The DHT11 module has 3 pins. Plugged into column E, rows 21-23.

WIRE 6

DHT + → left + rail (male-male)

Red wire from A21 (same row as + at E21) to the left + rail.

WIRE 7

DHT - → left - rail (male-male)

Black wire from A23 (same row as - at E23) to the left - rail.

WIRE 8

DHT DATA → A5 (GPIO 4) (male-male)

Green wire from A22 (same row as DATA at E22) to A5. Why A5? Because B5 = D4 (GPIO 4), and A5 is the free hole in that row.

7. Wire the BH1750 (light sensor)

The BH1750 has 5 pins. Plugged into column E, rows 27-30 (ADDR hangs off at row 30, unconnected). SDA at top.

WIRE 9

BH1750 VCC → left + rail (male-male)

Red wire from A30 (same row as VCC at E30) to the left + rail.

WIRE 10

BH1750 GND → left - rail (male-male)

Black wire from A29 (same row as GND at E29) to the left - rail.

WIRE 11

BH1750 SCL → A14 (GPIO 22) (male-male)

Yellow wire from A28 (same row as SCL at E28) to A14. Why A14? Because B14 = D22 (GPIO 22), and A14 is the free hole in that row.

WIRE 12

BH1750 SDA → A11 (GPIO 21) (male-male)

Yellow wire from A27 (same row as SDA at E27) to A11. Why A11? Because B11 = D21 (GPIO 21), and A11 is the free hole in that row.

What the arrows mean

W1←A1 = wire W1 goes FROM hole A1 TO the left + rail (pick any hole on the + strip).

W8→A5 = wire W8 arrives INTO hole A5 (coming from A23 where the DHT data is).

W5 on row 12 right side = male-female clip wire. Pin end in A20, socket clips onto the D34 pin sticking up from the ESP32.

9. Software setup

9A

Install Arduino IDE

Download from arduino.cc/en/software. It's free. Pick your OS. Install it.

9B

Add ESP32 board support

  1. Open Arduino IDE
  2. Go to File → Preferences
  3. In "Additional Board Manager URLs", paste:
    https://raw.githubusercontent.com/espressif/arduino-esp32/gh-pages/package_esp32_index.json
  4. Click OK
  5. Go to Tools → Board → Boards Manager
  6. Search "esp32"
  7. Install "esp32 by Espressif Systems"
9C

Install sensor libraries

Go to Sketch → Include Library → Manage Libraries

Search and install each of these (click "Install All" if asked about dependencies):

  1. "DHT sensor library" by Adafruit
  2. "Adafruit Unified Sensor" by Adafruit (may install automatically)
  3. "BH1750" by Christopher Laws
9D

Select board & port

  1. Go to Tools → Board → ESP32 Arduino → "ESP32 Dev Module"
  2. Plug in the ESP32 via USB
  3. Go to Tools → Port → pick the new one that appeared
    (usually /dev/cu.usbserial-... or /dev/cu.SLAB_USBtoUART on Mac)
No port showing? You may need a USB driver. Google "CP2102 driver" or "CH340 driver" for your OS. The chip is printed on the ESP32 board near the USB port.

10. Flash the firmware

10A

Open the sketch

In Arduino IDE, open firmware/orchid-sensor/orchid-sensor.ino

10B

Edit the config

At the top of the file, change these values:

const char* WIFI_SSID      = "YourWiFiName";
const char* WIFI_PASS      = "YourWiFiPassword";
const char* ENDPOINT       = "https://ewkfjmekrootyiijrgfh.supabase.co/functions/v1/sensor-reading";
const char* DEVICE_TOKEN   = "odev_YourTokenHere";

Get the device token by running the provisioning script (see the project README), or create one manually in Supabase.

10C

Upload

  1. Click the Upload button (→ arrow icon in the toolbar)
  2. Wait for "Compiling sketch..." then "Uploading..."
  3. If it says Connecting........_____ and fails: hold the BOOT button on the ESP32 while it's connecting, release when you see progress
10D

Monitor output

  1. Go to Tools → Serial Monitor
  2. Set baud rate to 115200 (dropdown at bottom right)
  3. You should see output like:
=== Orchid Sensor ===
Connecting to MyWiFi....
Connected! IP: 192.168.1.42
[Reading] Soil: 45% (raw: 2800) | Temp: 22.5°C | Humidity: 55.0% | Light: 350 lux
[OK] Posted successfully: {"success":true,"reading_id":"abc-123","plant_id":"def-456"}
If you see [OK] lines: Your sensor is live and sending data to Orchid. You're done with the hardware!

11. Calibrate the soil sensor

The soil sensor gives raw numbers (0-4095), not percentages. You need to tell it what "dry" and "wet" look like for YOUR specific sensor.

STEP

Enter calibration mode

  1. Hold the BOOT button on the ESP32 while pressing the RST (reset) button
  2. Release both — the Serial Monitor will show raw values every second for 60 seconds
  3. Hold the soil sensor in the air — note the number (e.g., 3650). That's your SOIL_DRY_VALUE.
  4. Dip the probe in a glass of water — note the number (e.g., 1600). That's your SOIL_WET_VALUE.
Do NOT submerge past the line! The soil sensor has a line where the probe ends and the electronics begin. Only dip the flat probe part into water or soil. Getting the electronics wet will destroy it.

Update the constants in the sketch and re-upload:

const int SOIL_DRY_VALUE   = 3650;  // YOUR air reading
const int SOIL_WET_VALUE   = 1600;  // YOUR water reading

12. Common mistakes

Wrong side of the gap. The center gap on the breadboard splits each row into two independent halves. If the ESP32 pin is on the LEFT side, your jumper wire must also go into the LEFT side of that same row.
Wrong GPIO pin. The numbers printed on the board are GPIO numbers, not physical pin numbers. Look at the silkscreen text on YOUR board — it might say "D34" or "34" or "G34" for GPIO 34.
Upload fails. Hold the BOOT button on the ESP32 while clicking Upload. Release when you see "Writing at 0x..." in the console. This is normal for many ESP32 boards.
DHT11 reads NaN. The data pin isn't connected properly, or you need to add a 10K resistor between DATA and VCC. The breakout module you ordered should have this built in — if you bought a bare sensor (4 pins, no board), you'll need the resistor.
BH1750 reads -1 or 0. The I2C connection isn't working. Double-check that SDA goes to GPIO 21 and SCL goes to GPIO 22 (not swapped). Also check that the ADDR pin is NOT connected to 3.3V.
WiFi won't connect. The ESP32 only supports 2.4 GHz WiFi, not 5 GHz. If your router broadcasts both, make sure you're connecting to the 2.4 GHz network. Also: SSID and password are case-sensitive.
